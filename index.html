Foundation
DME & Medical Insurance — The Basics
What we do, who we work with, and why Medicare Part B is our focus.
What is DME?
Durable Medical Equipment — reusable medical devices prescribed for home use. Braces, wheelchairs, oxygen equipment, CPAP machines.
Medicare Eligibility
Federal health insurance for US citizens. Eligible if age 65+ OR under 65 with qualifying disability. Must be US citizen or permanent resident.
Our Role
Telemarketing agency. We generate and qualify complete leads, then hand them to suppliers. We do not supply the equipment ourselves.
Key Focus — Medicare Part B: Covers doctor visits, outpatient care, and DME (braces). Parts A, C, and D are not relevant to our process.
Insurance Types We Skip
PPO, HMO, MSP, Medicaid-only, and Medicare Advantage (Part C). Any of these as primary = lead not eligible.
What Makes a Good Lead?
Active Medicare Part B · patient alive · no recent hospital/nursing home stay · no private plan as primary · valid PECOS-enrolled doctor with individual NPI.
Lead Structure
Required Information — Patient & Doctor
A lead = PT info + Doctor info. Both parts are mandatory. No exceptions.
Patient (PT) Info
1
Full Name + DOB
First & last name. Date of birth used for identity confirmation on every call.
2
MBI / MCN (Medicare ID)
11 alphanumeric characters. Example: 1EG4-TE5-MK73. Required for billing.
3
Physical Address
Must be a real street address. No PO Boxes. State & zip must match client criteria.
4
Measurements + Braces
Height, weight, waist. Shoe size only for ankle braces (BAB). Use exact brace codes.
Doctor Info
1
Full Name + Phone + Fax
Doctor's first & last name. Direct fax is critical — prior auth paperwork goes here.
2
NPI Number (Individual)
10-digit identifier. Must be Individual NPI — not Organizational. Example: 1234567893.
3
PECOS Enrollment
Must be enrolled in PECOS. Verify via NPI Registry. Not enrolled = claim denied.
4
Active State License
License must be valid and match the practice state. Expired or out-of-state = claim denial.
Organizational NPI is NOT accepted. Always verify the NPI belongs to the specific individual physician, not the clinic or hospital group.

Call Scripts
First Call — Confirming Mutuality
Speak slowly. Never leave an awkward silence. One second pause after the intro line.
Opening
Opening — Speak slowly, pause 1 second after intro "Hello, this is [Your Name] calling from Liberty Medical Supplies. I'm reaching out in regards to the mutual patient between us and Dr. [Doctor's Name]." (1-second pause)

"The name of the patient is [Patient's Name] and the date of birth is [Patient's DOB]."
After Confirming Patient is Active
After Confirmation — Get the fax number "Okay, so may I have the best fax number for the doctor? We need to send a prior authorization request for the patient that we need to be signed."
Closing — Based on GK Attitude
If GK Was Friendly "So by any chance, would the doctor be able to sign the paperwork by the end of the day?"
If GK Wasn't Super Nice "So roughly speaking, how long would it take for the provider to sign the paperwork?"
First Follow-Up
Follow-Up Call "Hi, this is [Your Name] calling from Liberty Medical Supplies. I'm reaching out to follow up on a fax we sent on [Date Fax Was Sent], just making sure it's been received from your side."

Then confirm: patient name, DOB, and purpose of the request — same as before.

Follow-Up Voicemail
Follow-Up VM — Include fax number and request "Hi, my name is [Your Name] from Liberty Medical Supplies. I'm reaching out regarding a fax I sent to Dr. [Doctor's Name] for a mutual patient, [Patient's Name], DOB [DOB]. If received, I kindly request the provider's signature along with the most recent office notes, faxed back to us at (888) 627-0067. For questions, call me at (331) 800-4684. Thank you, and have a blessed day."
Critical VM Steps: (1) Leave message clearly. (2) Wait for IVR to finish. (3) Press the required number to save VM. (4) Only hang up after completing step 3 — hanging up early = VM not received.
Rebuttals
Common Objections & How to Handle Them
Stay professional. Redirect, don't argue. Always aim to get the fax number.
Q
"Who are you with again?"
"With Liberty Medical Supplies. This call is regarding a prior authorization request we need to send to the doctor."
Q
"What medical supplies are you requesting?"
"The order was placed for [requested braces]." — If asked who placed it: "It was placed by Mr./Mrs. [Patient's Name]."
Q
"The doctor wouldn't be able to authorize such an order."
"It's totally fine — we're not seeking a signature. We're looking for the supervision of the doctor on the paperwork, regardless of what it is."
Q
"We cannot confirm whether the patient is active."
"Totally fine. We're just making sure the prior authorization is addressed correctly to the right physician. But anyways — can you provide the best fax number?"
Q
"Who ordered the brace?"
"The patient requested the brace and was referred to their doctor for evaluation. A medical provider must sign before anything can be dispensed, per HIPAA requirements."
Q
"How did the patient reach you / did you call the patient?"
"According to our system, the patient contacted us. We are a third-party provider — orders are placed by the patient, usually under the guidance of their PCP or another doctor."
Common Cases — Part 1
Real Situations & Best Responses
Scenarios you'll face daily — handle them with confidence.
!
Doctor is on vacation.
  • Ask if a covering doctor or PA can sign instead.
  • If not, note when the doctor is back and chase another doctor in the meantime.
!
Doctor treats area X, but brace is for area Y.
  • Explain the patient requested braces for both — ask if they can sign for the area they treat.
  • Always verify before faxing if the doctor covers the specific area.
!
Fax sent but no response after multiple follow-ups.
  • Try medical records or nurse directly. Leave a message noting urgency.
  • Follow up for 2–3 weeks. If still nothing → escalate, look for another doctor.
!
Patient not active with this doctor — no other doctors listed.
  • Ask GK if patient was referred elsewhere.
  • Call the patient directly — introduce as from a doctor's office, not a supply company.
!
Office says they didn't receive the fax.
  • Ask for an alternative fax or email. Try other departments.
  • Offer to resend while they're on the line.
Common Cases — Part 2
More Scenarios & Solutions
Edge cases that require extra care — stay consistent.
!
GK says no mention of a brace in the chart.
  • Explain the patient requested it recently — may not have come up in prior visits.
  • Ask if the provider can evaluate and add a diagnosis. Offer to resend paperwork.
!
GK says patient didn't request the brace.
  • Agree they can confirm with the patient. Once confirmed, offer to resend paperwork.
  • Leave the lead 2 days, call back trying a different GK. Pursue other doctors in parallel.
!
Doctor signed but chart notes are missing.
  • Contact medical records directly — state you have a signed order and need supporting docs.
  • If needed, call another doctor and explain the situation to obtain compliant documentation.
!
GK refuses to provide any info — including fax number.
  • Call back later and try a different GK or doctor directly.
  • Search the fax number online. Call back asking only for the fax, no full intro.
!
Patient is now seeing a PA — not the original doctor.
  • If form already sent: ask office to forward to PA. Note their name + NPI.
  • If form not sent yet: request the PA's fax number and send directly.
!
GK says paperwork must go to PCP or specialist.
  • Ask for the PCP's name and send directly to them.
  • If PCP denied it, explain the patient referred you to this doctor as part of their treatment plan.

  • Active Medicare Part B — not just Part A
  • No recent hospital or nursing home stay
  • No primary PPO, HMO, or MSP coverage

Workflow
The Full Chasing Process — Step by Step
From receiving a lead to getting a signed order. Every step matters.
1
Receive Lead + Data Validation
Check all PT and doctor fields. Run PECOS, NPI, S&S, and eligibility checks. Mark lead as Good or flag issues before proceeding.
2
First Call — Confirm Mutuality
Call the doctor's office. Confirm the patient is active. Introduce as Liberty Medical Supplies. Get the fax number. Ask about signing timeline.
3
Send the Fax (Prior Auth Request)
Fax the prior authorization paperwork to the confirmed fax number. Log the exact date and time of transmission.
4
Follow-Up Calls (Every 1–2 Days)
Check if fax was received. Ask for signature status. If no response after 2–3 weeks → escalate. Try other departments or another doctor.
5
Obtain Signed DO + Chart Notes (CN)
Doctor's Order must be signed. Most cases also require recent office/chart notes (CN) showing medical necessity. Request from medical records if needed.
6
Submit to Processing
Signed DO + chart notes received → file moves to Processing for final submission to the supplier and Medicare billing.

Acceptable Specialties
Doctor Specialties — Who Can Sign
Know what each specialty treats, when they're valid, and why — before you fax.
PCP Primary Care / Family Medicine / Internal Medicine Accepted
In the US, the PCP (also called "family doctor" or "general practitioner") is usually the first point of contact for patients. They manage overall health, refer to specialists, and see patients regularly for chronic conditions — making them one of our most valuable sign sources.
Treats: General health, chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, arthritis, back pain), preventive care. They commonly treat patients who need back, knee, wrist, or ankle braces.
Internal Medicine physicians specialize in adult diseases (18+). In practice, they function like PCPs and are fully valid for DME orders.
Ortho Orthopedic Doctor / Orthopedic Surgeon Accepted
Treats: Bones, joints, muscles, tendons, ligaments, and spine. This is the strongest specialty for our product category. They directly treat the exact body parts our braces support — knees, back, shoulders, wrists, ankles, elbows, hips.
Medicare and insurers fully accept orthopedic orders for braces. An ortho referral on the chart is a strong medical necessity indicator. Important: verify they treat the specific joint in question before faxing.
Sports Med Sports Medicine Accepted
Treats: Musculoskeletal injuries, joint pain, sprains, strains, overuse injuries. Very valid for knee, ankle, wrist, and shoulder braces. Sports medicine doctors also treat non-athletes — many older Medicare patients visit them for joint pain management.
Often works alongside orthopedics. Accepted by Medicare for DME orders when they're the treating provider for the relevant condition.
Pain Mgmt Pain Management Accepted
Treats: Chronic pain conditions — back pain, neck pain, joint pain, nerve pain (neuropathy). Very relevant for BB (back brace), NB (neck brace), and knee braces.
Pain management physicians actively document patient complaints in chart notes — making their CN (chart notes) especially useful. If the patient sees pain management, chart notes often clearly state the diagnosis and pain levels needed for Medicare medical necessity.
Neuro / Rheum Neurology / Rheumatology Accepted
Neurology treats: Nerve conditions, neuropathy, MS, Parkinson's, radiculopathy (nerve root compression). Back and ankle braces are often prescribed for patients with nerve damage affecting mobility or stability.
Rheumatology treats: Arthritis (RA, OA), lupus, gout, fibromyalgia, joint inflammation. Extremely relevant for knee, wrist, and ankle braces. Rheumatologists frequently treat Medicare patients with chronic joint conditions.
NP / PA Nurse Practitioner / Physician Assistant Conditional
Allowed ONLY if they work in orthopedic, sports medicine, or musculoskeletal practices. Medicare recognizes NPs and PAs as eligible ordering providers for DME if they have prescriptive authority in their state and are PECOS-enrolled.
Not suitable if they work in: Diabetes clinics, cardiology, mental health, or any practice unrelated to musculoskeletal conditions. The specialty of their supervising physician / practice matters — always verify.
DPM Podiatry (DPM — Doctor of Podiatric Medicine) Foot/Ankle Only
Treats: Foot and ankle conditions exclusively — bunions, plantar fasciitis, heel pain, ankle instability, diabetic foot complications. Common in the US, especially among diabetic Medicare patients.
Only valid for BAB (Both Ankle Braces) — L1902/L1970. Cannot sign for knee, back, wrist, or any other brace. If the patient has a podiatrist listed but needs a back brace, you need a different doctor.
PT / OT / Chiro Physical Therapists, Occupational Therapists, Chiropractors Not Allowed
Medicare does not recognize these providers as eligible to order DME. A chiropractor (DC — Doctor of Chiropractic) is licensed to adjust the spine but is not a medical doctor and cannot prescribe or order Medicare-billable DME. Same applies to PT and OT.
Even if a PT or chiropractor signs a form, the claim will be denied by Medicare. Never pursue these providers as signatories. If the patient only sees a chiropractor, ask if they have a PCP or orthopedic doctor.
Document Structure
Documents We Send & What They Contain
Every package must start with a Cover Page. Know each page before you send.
1
Cover Page Always First
Introduces the request and summarizes the patient. This is the first page the office sees — must be clean, professional, and clearly identify the patient and requesting provider.
2
RX / DO — Doctor Order (Prescription) Core · Must Sign
The core document. Contains two main boxes: Patient Information and Doctor Information. The Diagnosis section only needs: (a) affected area/joints (e.g., Both Ankles, Lower Back) and (b) the L-Code for the specific brace version (e.g., L0456). This is the document the doctor signs.
3
Medical Necessity Optional
Additional diagnosis/supporting page that elaborates on why the brace is medically necessary. Can be signed but is not mandatory. Helps reinforce the claim if chart notes are thin.
4
Addendum Optional
Extra supporting documentation. Can be signed but not mandatory. Provides additional context for complex or multi-brace orders.
5
CN — Chart Notes / Clinical Notes Mandatory
Must be received alongside the signed RX/DO. Must have an active/recent date (within 5 months and 10 days). Should clearly document the patient's pain and condition. Request from medical records if not received with the signed DO.
Both the signed DO and the CN (Chart Notes) are required to complete the order. Incomplete or outdated documents will be rejected by the client.
Client Acceptance Criteria
Document Rules — What the Client Requires
These are hard rules. Anything outside these limits gets rejected — no exceptions.
Doctor Order (DO / RX)
1
Good Quality
Must be received clearly — no cutting, no interruptions, fully legible. Blurry or cut-off signatures are rejected.
2
Signed Date Within 2 Months
The date the doctor signs the DO must be within the last 2 months (maximum). Older signatures = automatic rejection.
3
All Information Correct
Patient info, doctor info, L-codes, and diagnosis must all be accurate. No wrong submissions — even small errors cause rejection.
Chart Notes (CN)
1
Visit Date Within 5 Months + 10 Days
The date of the office visit documented in the chart notes must be within 5 months and 10 days. Older notes = rejected.
2
Clear Diagnosis Present
Must contain a clear diagnosis regarding the patient's pain or complaint. Vague notes like "patient visit" without a specific condition are not accepted.
Summary: DO signed within 2 months · CN dated within 5 months 10 days · both documents clear and complete · all info accurate. Meet all criteria → order moves to processing. Miss any one → rejection.
DO Date Limit
Max 2 months from the date the doctor signed. If a lead has been chasing too long, the signature may expire — request a re-sign.
CN Date Limit
Max 5 months + 10 days from the visit date. Older chart notes won't qualify. If expired, ask for a new office visit or more recent notes.
Both Required
Having only the DO or only the CN is not enough. Both must be present, within date, and accurate before submitting to the client.
